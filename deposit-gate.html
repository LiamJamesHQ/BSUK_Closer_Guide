How to Use This

  1. This is a shape, not a script to recite. The three beats are the argument. Learn them, then say them in your own words, in your own order, wherever they fit the conversation.
  2. The gate goes after first human contact. Never before. This is the one rule that can't be bent — see Card 5. Lead with the deposit and you'll scare off the buyers you most want.
  3. The honesty is the pitch. The strongest line in here is the admission that we've let people wait. Don't smooth it out. It's what makes everything after it believable.
One · The one-liner, for you

"The deposit isn't me protecting my money. It's me protecting my hours — so the people who are actually buying get the attention they deserve."

Why it matters

Every hour spent designing for someone who was never going to buy is an hour stolen from someone who already has.

That isn't a discipline problem. At six or seven consultations a day, it's arithmetic.

Half of this job is convincing yourself. The gate isn't a hardening of the business — it's what makes it possible to be generous with the people who are real.
Two

The Three-Beat Story

The argument underneath everything else
Beat one

The easy one dies quietly

A customer wants a round wood-burning hot tub. Standard item. Nothing bespoke. She messages after a call.

It's read in ten minutes. It isn't answered.

She has to chase. Twelve days later she still hasn't got a clean price or a clear photo. She writes: a bit disappointed.

That sale was never hard. It was five grand of stock. It died in the gap between "read" and "replied."

Beat two

The big one stalls even when you win it

A multi-room build. Thirty-three minute call, site visit, verbal yes, deposit sent. Won.

And then it stops — because the plan, the price and the first-fix sheet all live in one person's head and one person's spare evenings.

The deposit worked. What came after it didn't.

Beat three

Now times it by ten

One missed message. One stalled build. Now run ten of those at once, alongside the calls, the quotes, the workshop.

It stops being about effort.

It becomes physically impossible.

Not "I need to try harder." There aren't enough hours in the day to give every enquiry a full custom design.

The landing: So we qualify. The £500 tells us who's real. The real ones get the hours.
Three

The Two Worked Examples

Anonymised — safe to say in front of a prospect
The one we lost

"We had a lady after a straightforward wood-fired tub. Nothing complicated — a standard build. She messaged me, I read it, and I didn't get back to her properly for nearly two weeks because I was buried in calls. By the time I did, she'd gone cold.

And that one was easy. That's the one that stings."

The one we won

"Then there's a big multi-room project. Proper build, big spend. He'd had the full call, come out to site, said yes. But between him saying yes and me getting the plans over, it just sat — because I was doing the plans myself, at night, between everything else.

He waited. He shouldn't have had to."

The turn

"Now imagine ten of those running at once. That's where I am. So we changed how we do it."

Why real beats hypothetical: a made-up example sounds like a sales technique. A real one you're clearly still annoyed about sounds like the truth — because it is. Keep the irritation in your voice.
Four

The Reframe — What the £500 Actually Is

Not a fee. Not a barrier. A place in the queue.

It comes off your build. It isn't an extra charge — it's the first £500 of what you were going to pay anyway.

Once it's in, you're not an enquiry any more. You're a buyer. That flips how we work. You go to the front.

You get the real design hours. Full plans, proper drawings, a full presentation — not a rushed sketch between calls.

It's a small commitment made after we've given you a plan and a price. You know what you're buying before you pay a penny.

We're a workshop, not a warehouse. There's nothing on a shelf. We build yours. The deposit is what turns a conversation into a build slot.

The move: never describe it as a cost. It's an upgrade in how they get treated. The price of the sauna hasn't changed — only when they pay part of it.
Five

The Guardrail — Sequencing

The rule that stops the gate scaring off good buyers
Non-negotiable

The deposit ask comes AFTER first human contact.
Never before.

Nobody pays £500 to a stranger. They pay it to someone who's already given them something.

  1. Call — real conversation, understand the project
  2. Ballpark plan and price — free, no obligation
  3. Site visit or show site, if it's a big one
  4. Then the deposit ask — to unlock the full design
  5. Full plans, presentation, detailed spec

The big multi-room build only agreed after the 33-minute call and the site visit. He'd been given value first. That's why it landed.

The move: never lead with the deposit. Earn it in the first fifteen minutes, then ask. The gate isn't a filter at the door — it's a filter after the tour.
Six

Spoken Version — For Calls

Say it — don't read it
Word for word

"Right — let me be straight with you about how we run this, because it'll make sense of what I'm about to ask.

From this chat I can give you a solid idea of what your build looks like and what it costs. That bit's free, no obligation. You'll walk away knowing your number either way.

Where it changes is the full design — the detailed plans, the drawings, the proper presentation. That's real hours from our team.

And I'll be honest with you. We're doing six or seven of these consultations a day. I've had customers I genuinely wanted to help wait too long, because I was buried designing for people who were never going to build. That's on the system, not on them, and I've fixed it.

So here's how it works now. Once you're happy with the plan and the price, it's a £500 deposit to lock it in. That's not a fee — it comes straight off your build. What it does is secure your place in the queue and put your project at the front, so we can put the real design hours into it knowing it's a live build.

The moment that's in, you're not an enquiry any more. You're a buyer, and you get treated like one."

Delivery notes

Deliver it after you've given them the ballpark. Never before.

The honesty about being buried is the strongest bit. Don't smooth it out — it's what makes them believe the rest.

Don't apologise for the gate. State it like a fact, not a request. If you sound like you're asking permission, they'll hear a negotiation.

If they hesitate: "Totally fine — take the plan and the price away, have a think. Nothing to pay for that." Then follow up. Hesitation isn't a no.

Short version — thread already warm

Right — once you're happy with the plan and the price, it's a £500 deposit to lock it in. That comes straight off the build, and it secures your slot in the queue.

That's when the proper design work starts — full plans, drawings, the lot. It's how we make sure the hours go into live builds. 👍

The move: end on a question, not the ask. "Happy to get the plan and price over first?" gives them an easy yes that isn't the £500 — and once they've said yes to something, the deposit is a smaller step.
Eight

The Three Pushbacks

Have the answer ready — don't improvise it
They sayYou say
"Why should I pay before I've seen a design?" "You won't be. You'll have a plan and a price before anything's paid — that's free. The deposit only unlocks the detailed design."
"What if I change my mind?" "It's non-refundable, but it comes off your build and it holds your slot. That's why we only ask once you're happy with the number — nobody's paying blind."
"Nobody else asks for this." "We're not a shop, we're a manufacturer. There's nothing on a shelf — we build yours. The deposit is what turns it into a build slot."
The move: every pushback is answered by pointing back at the sequence. They've already had something free. The deposit only buys what comes next.
Nine

What Happens the Second They Pay

So you can say it with confidence

The moment the £500 lands, automatically:

— They get an email within seconds — deposit received, place secured, what happens next
— They get an SMS confirming the same
— You get a notification to call them within 24 hours
— Marta gets a notification to reconcile the payment
— They move to Deposit Paid in the pipeline

You don't have to remember any of it. That's the point.
